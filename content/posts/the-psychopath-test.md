## I.

“P/E on Nxt FY: 27.5X” was the last line in the research report from Goldman Sachs, dated September 19, 1996. This cruel line led to the death of Shubuta as “Chainsaw” Dunlap's ruthless restructuring at Sunbeam. He fired 11,200 workers, about 35 percent of the work force. Investors were expected to celebrate Sunbeam’s mass layoffs, a remorseless view of the lives behind those numbers. Dunlap himself becomes one of the emblematic examples in the book where corporate ruthlessness is weighed against Bob Hare’s checklist of psychopathy.

The book begins like a mystery story, with a puzzle that draws Ronson into the larger theme of what counts as madness, and who gets to decide. It examines corporate psychopaths like Dunlap, the story of Tony who trapped in Broadmoor psychiatric hospital after scoring too high on Hare’s test, the rise of David Shayler, the ex-MI5 agent whose conspiracy theories turned him into a messianic figure, along with others living on the blurred boundary between sanity and insanity.

## II.

Television producers have perfected their own method of mining madness for profit. They hunt for people with just the right degree of instability. Scott, who worked as an assistant producer, admitted:

> “I'd ask them what medication they were on. They'd give me a list. Then I'd go to a medical website to see what the medications were for. And I'd assess if they were too mad to come onto the show or just mad enough.”

They learned to “pick the bits that are mad enough, ignore the bits that aren’t, and then leave”, intuitively knowing who would make good television. The result is that every prime time program is filled with guests offering the perfect dose of craziness to keep audiences entertained.

This systematic exploitation reveals something disturbing about our collective psychology. We've created an appetite for the right sort of madness, people “who are a bit madder than we fear we're becoming, and in a recognizable way. We might be anxious but we aren't as anxious as they are. We might be paranoid but we aren't as paranoid as they are. We are entertained by them, and comforted that we're not as mad as they are.” But this comfort comes at the cost of genuine human connection.

## III.

Meanwhile, pharmaceutical companies have found their own profitable angle in this business. They aggressively market psychiatric medications to children, expanding the definition of mental illness to create new markets. As psychiatrist Allen Frances warns,

> “Psychiatric diagnoses are getting closer and closer to the boundary of normal. That boundary is very populous. The most crowded boundary is the boundary with normal.”

The drug companies benefit from this diagnostic creep, turning normal childhood behaviors into treatable conditions.

The system creates victims at every level. There are obviously a lot of ill people out there.

> “But there are also people in the middle, getting overlabeled, becoming nothing more than a big splurge of madness in the minds of the people who benefit from it.”

People become so desperate to escape modern pressures that they secretly will a nervous breakdown on so they can get admitted to a psychiatric hospital. Does people in the middle shouldn't necessarily be defined by their maddest edges?

## IV.

Our corporate culture celebrates its own version of acceptable madness. The same traits that condemn Tony to Dangerous and Severe Personality Disorder (DSPD) Unit are the very ones that elevate Dunlap to corporate stardom. We’ve accepted business cultures where efficiency is prized above empathy, and people learn to suppress compassion in order to meet quotas, close deals, or climb the corporate ladder. As Ronson observes,

> “the difference between a psychopath in Broadmoor and a psychopath on Wall Street was the luck of being born into a stable, rich family.”

The most disturbing realization is that we weren't only in the business of madness, we were also in the business of conformity. We’ve built a culture that pushes people to fit in and shows less and less tolerance for difference. Yet paradoxically, for some, receiving a label is better. It can confer a sense of hope and direction. We've made being diagnosed preferable to being different.

## V.

This entire industry operates on a principle of emotional detachment that may be infecting us all.

> “Madness has spiraled into something too outlandish, too out-of-the-ballpark, and consequently useless. We don't want obvious exploitation. We want smoke-and-mirrors exploitation.”

We've learned to consume suffering while maintaining distance from it, without acknowledging our role in creating it.

Perhaps our collective obsession with identifying and profiting from madness is itself a symptom of societal dysfunction. A lot of people are scared they're going mad, and in a culture where the pull toward madness often feels stronger than the pull toward sanity, we may have lost our ability to distinguish between genuine mental illness and manufactured dysfunction. The real madness lies not in the people we label and exploit, but in our willingness to build an entire economy around their pain while pretending we remain untouched by the moral corruption such a system demands.
