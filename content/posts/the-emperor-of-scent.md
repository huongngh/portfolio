## Luca Turin

At the heart of the book is Luca Turin, a biophysicist with an extraordinary nose and an even more extraordinary theory. Turin isn't your typical scientist. He's a man who can wax poetic about the scent of a particular perfume while simultaneously delving into the quantum mechanics that might explain how our noses detect these odors.

His theory — that smell works by detecting molecular vibrations rather than molecular shape — is fascinating. It's like a real-life scientific mystery, complete with skeptics, supporters, and a whole lot of sniffing. But what really got me was Turin's passion. He paints pictures with words, describing scents in terms of colors, textures, and emotions.

There was this:

> **57 for Her (Chevignon) ★**
>
> Chevignon is a fashion house of such toe-curling vulgarity that one finds oneself hoping that they will never come up with a good fragrance, for one would then have to praise it. Fortunately, that possibility remains as remote as ever. 57 for Her is a sad little thing, an incongruous dried-prunes note with a metallic edge that manages the rare feat of being at once cloying and harsh.

And this:

> **Paradox (Jacomo) ★★★★★**
>
> Beauty itself, as with faces, is not simple: perfumes can be handsome (Mitsouko), graceful (Calandre), gorgeous (Joy), comely (Shalimar), radiant (Tommy Girl), exquisite (Apres I'Ondee), stunning (Angel). Reader beware: Paradox is, to paraphrase something once said about Scriabin's music, a perfume of "almost unbearable loveliness." One of the properties of loveliness is that it disarms all attempts to be serious, and turns all critical machinery into a pile of whirring junk.

## The Vibration Theory

Did you know that humans can detect over a trillion different scents? That's more than the number of colors we can see or sounds we can hear. Odor molecules fit into receptors like keys into locks — like a microscopic dance. Or at least, that's what we thought before Turin came along.

Turin proposes that our noses work more like spectroscopes than lock-and-key mechanisms. Instead of detecting the shape of molecules, he suggests our noses pick up on the vibrations of the bonds between atoms. It's a radical idea, one that flies in the face of established scientific thought.

Burr does an excellent job of explaining the science in a way that's accessible to non-experts. He compares the traditional lock-and-key model to a vending machine — only coins of the right shape will work. Turin's theory, on the other hand, is more like a piano, where different molecules produce different "notes" that our noses detect.

---

## The Controversy

It's not often that you get to see a scientific paradigm potentially shift in real time. Burr doesn't shy away from the skepticism — and sometimes outright hostility — that Turin faced from the scientific establishment.

It's a reminder that science isn't always a smooth, linear progression. Sometimes it's messy. Sometimes revolutionary ideas are met with resistance. Turin's battle to get his theory recognized is a testament to the perseverance required in scientific inquiry — and a stark reminder of how personal and political science can be. Scientists are human, after all, with their own biases, rivalries, and vested interests.

## The Unexpected Detours

There's a whole section about the smell of aldehydes — basically just chains of carbon atoms: four carbons, five carbons, six carbons, and so on. They have strange behaviors. All the aldehydes with an even number of carbons smelled like mandarin orange. But all the odd ones smelled like smoky candles. Chanel No. 5, one of the world's greatest perfumes, used almost entirely the even aldehydes, with just a touch of the odd.

Then there's the bizarre world of synthetic musks. These compounds have a strange property: some people can smell them, while others simply cannot. A real-life example of how dramatically smell perception varies from person to person.

---

## Why This Book Stayed With Me

At its core, *The Emperor of Scent* isn't just about perfume or even about smell. It's about passion, curiosity, and the relentless pursuit of understanding. It's about looking at the world differently, questioning assumptions, and finding beauty in unexpected places.

Highly recommend it — especially if you've ever stopped to wonder why a single scent can pull you back to a memory you forgot you had.
